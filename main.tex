\begin{abstract}

%Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is central to modern data science, yet current EDA systems rely on explicit user feedback, example validation and active learning that disrupts cognitive flow and captures only a narrow view of analytical interest. We argue that implicit behavioral signals from natural exploration provide a richer, more authentic basis for recommendation. However, evaluating implicit recommenders poses its own challenge: realistic workloads produce high inter-query overlap that renders all methods statistically indistinguishable, masking differences in recommender capability. This paper makes four contributions: (1) a systematic critique of explicit feedback paradigms in EDA, showing their cognitive overhead and analytical limitations; (2) a workload recommender alignment theory that formally characterises when each class of recommender succeeds or fails, yielding a prescriptive recipe for constructing controlled benchmarks; (3) a Multi-Dimensional Interestingness (MDI) recommender that combines association rules, diversity measures, and novelty detection through temporal accumulation; and (4) a complementary evaluation methodology pairing realistic visualization-driven sessions (ecological validity) with a controlled adversarial benchmark (construct validity). On the adversarial benchmark, MDI achieves 36\% higher F1 than random selection, while on realistic drill-down workloads all methods perform similarly demonstrating that principled evaluation methodology is as important as recommender design.
Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is central to modern data science, yet most systems still rely on explicit user feedback, validation, or active learning. These mechanisms interrupt cognitive flow and capture only a narrow slice of analysts' true interests. We argue for two paradigm shifts in how database systems (DBMS) support EDA: (1) from explicit to implicit interest signals inferred from natural exploration behaviour, and (2) from purely ``ecologically valid'' benchmarks to evaluation schemes that also achieve construct validity by probing specific recommender capabilities.
We formalise a workload-recommender alignment framework that characterises when different classes of recommenders succeed or fail, and show how it yields prescriptive recipes for constructing diagnostic benchmarks. To ground this vision, we instantiate an association-aware implicit recommender, Multi-Dimensional Interestingness (MDI), and a synthetic benchmark that tests the ability to detect embedded associations under sparse, skewed signals. Experiments on SIMBA-generated drill-down workloads and our controlled benchmark show that realistic workloads alone cannot distinguish recommenders, whereas construct-valid benchmarks reveal substantial performance gaps (MDI achieves $\sim$36\% higher F1 than random in our setup). We conclude by outlining research challenges in learning, explaining, and standardising implicit interest models and diagnostic benchmarks for human-in-the-loop data systems.

\end{abstract}

Exploratory data analysis (EDA) sits at the centre of modern data science workflows, as organisations accumulate increasingly large and heterogeneous datasets~\cite{idc2019data}. The database community has responded with interactive exploration systems offering visual interfaces, query-by-example paradigms, and active learning~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016, shen2014discovering, rezig2021dice}. These systems advance beyond traditional query-answer models, but largely preserve one key assumption: that analytical interest must be elicited through \emph{explicit} user feedback.

Existing exploration systems ask analysts to rank tuples, validate examples, or answer Boolean membership queries~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016, shen2014discovering, rezig2021dice}. Such interactions impose context switches between reasoning about the data and judging recommendation quality, introducing measurable cognitive overhead and disrupting analytical flow~\cite{monsell2003task, rubinstein2001executive}. At the same time, most systems optimise for similarity in feature space rather than analytical interest, and often rely on a single interestingness criterion despite empirical evidence that what analysts find interesting is contextual and multi-dimensional~\cite{somechPredictingWhatInteresting2019, chansonInterestingnessMeasuresExploratory2025a}.

We instead envision EDA systems that \emph{infer} analytical interest from \emph{implicit} behavioural signals: the queries analysts write, the results they inspect, and the sequences in which they follow up on emerging patterns. Implicit feedback has proved powerful in other recommendation domains~\cite{hu2008collaborative, kelly2003implicit, koren2009collaborative}, and the temporal structure of exploration sessions provides a natural basis for modelling evolving analytical goals without interrupting the analyst.

However, shifting to implicit recommenders raises a second, equally important question: \emph{how should we evaluate whether a recommender understands analytical patterns, rather than exploiting artifacts of the workload?} We show that realistic visualisation-driven workloads, such as SIMBA-style drill-down sessions, exhibit high inter-query overlap due to progressive refinement. Under such overlap, a wide range of recommenders achieve statistically similar scores, making them effectively indistinguishable. Evaluating solely on realistic workloads can therefore overstate the effectiveness of simple baselines while obscuring methodological advances. In this setting, principled evaluation methodology is as important as recommender design.

We argue for two paradigm shifts in how DBMSs support EDA:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1em, topsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{From explicit to implicit interest modelling.} We critique explicit feedback paradigms in EDA, grounding a vision of systems that infer multi-dimensional analytical interest from natural exploration behaviour rather than interrupting analysts with rating tasks (Section~\ref{sec:implicit}).
  \item \textbf{From ecological-only to construct-valid evaluation.} We introduce a \emph{workload--recommender alignment} framework that characterises when different recommender classes succeed or fail and prescribes how to construct diagnostic benchmarks that test specific capabilities (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
\end{itemize}

To make this vision concrete, we provide one instantiation along both axes: \fcirc{1} an association-aware implicit recommender, \emph{Multi-Dimensional Interestingness} (MDI), which combines association rules, diversity, and novelty through temporal accumulation as an exemplar of the broader design space (Section~\ref{sec:mdi}); and
\fcirc{2} a controlled benchmark that embeds sparse association-rule signal under skewed, overlapping workloads, illustrating how construct-valid evaluation can differentiate recommenders even when realistic workloads cannot (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
These instantiations are not the endpoint, but rather evidence that implicit interest modelling and diagnostic benchmark design form a promising research agenda for human-in-the-loop data systems.

\subsection{Limitations of Explicit Feedback in EDA}
Most current EDA systems share a common assumption: analytical interest can be elicited explicitly and approximated by similarity to previously explored data. Explore-by-example systems~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016, mottin2014exemplar, bonifati2014interactive} optimise for tuple similarity or pattern matching, conflating feature-space proximity with cognitive relevance. Query-by-example systems such as YMALDB~\cite{drosouYmaldbExploringRelational2013} require users to specify similarity criteria and repeatedly assess recommendation quality, while faceted search systems like DICE~\cite{rezig2021dice, el2016dice} enable structured exploration but still depend on explicit validation of results.

Empirical studies demonstrate that no single interestingness measure consistently predicts what analysts find interesting across domains and tasks~\cite{somechPredictingWhatInteresting2019}. Experiments on financial, scientific, and social media datasets reveal that correlation-based measures perform well in some contexts, while clustering-based measures excel in others~\cite{chansonInterestingnessMeasuresExploratory2025a}. This heterogeneity suggests that effective EDA systems must embrace \emph{multi-dimensional} interest modelling rather than relying on a single metric.

Despite their differences, these systems rely on explicit feedback in various forms: tuple rankings~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016}, example validation~\cite{rezig2021dice}, Boolean membership queries~\cite{bonifati2014interactive}, or similarity assessments~\cite{mottin2014exemplar}. Each interaction forces analysts to step out of the natural flow of exploration to assess recommendation quality. Cognitive psychology studies show that such context switches impose measurable overhead and degrade problem-solving effectiveness~\cite{monsell2003task, rubinstein2001executive}. As exploration deepens, the cumulative cost of explicit feedback scales poorly, potentially discouraging its use when it is most informative.

\vspace{-0.5em}
\subsection{Implicit Signals as a Richer Alternative}

We argue that implicit feedback derived from natural exploration behaviour provides a richer and more reliable basis for modelling interest than explicit rankings or ratings. This view rests on three observations:

\noindent \fcirc{1} \textbf{Cognitive flow preservation.}
Explicit feedback interleaves exploration and evaluation, increasing extraneous cognitive load. Cognitive load theory~\cite{sweller1988cognitive} explains that exploratory analysis already imposes substantial intrinsic load; additional rating or labelling tasks can overwhelm working memory. Implicit feedback, by contrast, preserves flow by inferring interest from behaviours analysts exhibit, such as follow-up queries or drill-down operations.

\noindent \fcirc{2} \textbf{Behavioural authenticity.}
Implicit signals reflect genuine analytical decisions: when an analyst formulates a follow-up query or investigates a subset of results, these actions embody authentic expressions of interest unconstrained by rating scales~\cite{kelly2003implicit}. Rather than asking users to \emph{describe} what they find interesting, systems can \emph{observe} what they pursue.

\noindent \fcirc{3} \textbf{Temporal richness.}
Implicit feedback carries temporal structure that reveals how interests evolve~\cite{koren2009collaborative}. Short-term signals (which tuples or regions are accessed first) indicate immediate priorities, while longer-term patterns (which investigation threads persist across queries) expose higher-level goals. Pirolli's information foraging theory~\cite{pirolli2005information} predicts that analysts follow information-scent gradients; the resulting trails of queries and interactions provide a rich signal of perceived value.

In this paper, we operationalise implicit interest using structural signals readily available in database logs: sequences of queries and the overlap of their result sets. This is a conservative first step that enables us to study methodological challenges in a setting where ground-truth can be controlled. Our broader vision extends to richer behavioural traces from visual front-ends (e.g., clicks, hovers, dwell times), which we discuss as an important avenue for future work.

This finding has important implications. Prior work that evaluates recommenders exclusively on realistic workloads may overstate the effectiveness of simple baselines while undervaluing methods that capture more nuanced analytical structure. Existing EDA benchmarks such as IDEBench~\cite{eichmannIDEBenchBenchmarkInteractive2020} were designed primarily to measure DBMS performance under interactive workloads, not to differentiate recommender quality; high overlap in such workloads can mask genuine differences in capability.

\vspace{-0.5em}
\subsection{The Need for Construct Validity in EDA Benchmarks}

To reason about benchmark adequacy, we distinguish two complementary notions of validity~\cite{shadish2002experimental}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1em, topsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Ecological validity}: Do benchmark sessions resemble real analyst behaviour? SIMBA provides this: its progressive drill-down and visualisation-driven queries are representative of common analytical workflows.
  \item \textbf{Construct validity}: Does the benchmark actually test the capability it claims to measure? SIMBA \emph{lacks} this for recommender evaluation, because high overlap conflates genuine pattern recognition with simple statistical coincidence. A method can perform well without truly modelling analytical interest.
\end{itemize}


Other fields address this tension by complementing naturalistic datasets with \emph{diagnostic} benchmarks that test specific properties. In computer vision, CLEVR~\cite{johnson2017clevr} uses synthetic scenes to diagnose whether visual question-answering models perform compositional reasoning or exploit dataset biases. In NLP, CheckList~\cite{ribeiro2020beyond} provides behavioural tests that probe specific linguistic capabilities, and adversarial NLI benchmarks~\cite{nie2020adversarial} test whether entailment models rely on annotation artefacts.

We adopt the same principle for EDA recommender evaluation. Our controlled benchmark (detailed in Section~\ref{sec:theory:recipe}) tests a specific, method-agnostic property: \emph{can the recommender detect association rules embedded in the data when superficial shortcuts are removed?} The benchmark is constructed by deriving conditions where each baseline class provably fails, then instantiating workloads that satisfies all these conditions. The derivation yields a general alignment framework, which we present as a blueprint for designing construct-valid benchmarks beyond our specific instantiation.

\section{Multi-Dimensional Interestingness Recommender}
\label{sec:mdi}

%The theoretical framework in Section~\ref{sec:theory} defines the MDI scoring function formally (Eq.~\ref{eq:mdi-score}) and shows that its temporal accumulation property gives it an advantage over memoryless baselines when the workload contains sparse association-rule signal. Here we describe the intuition behind each component and the design choices involved.

The workload-recommender alignment framework in Section~\ref{sec:theory} highlights when temporally accumulating, association-aware models have an advantage over memoryless baselines, particularly under sparse, skewed signal. We now describe one concrete instantiation of this class: the \emph{Multi-Dimensional Interestingness} (MDI) recommender. MDI is not intended as the final word on interest modelling, but as an exemplar of how multiple signals can be combined to infer implicit analytical interest over time.

At a high level, MDI assigns each tuple $t$ in the current result $R_q$ a score defined as:
\vspace{-0.5em}
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{MDI}}(t, q)
  = \alpha \cdot A(t, q) + \beta \cdot D(t, q) + \gamma \cdot N(t, q),
  \label{eq:mdi-score}
\end{equation}
where $A(t,q)$ captures association evidence, $D(t,q)$ captures diversity contributions, $N(t,q)$ captures novelty, and $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1$.

\vspace{-0.5em}
\subsection{Four Dimensions of Analytical Interest}

Drawing on Hilderman and Hamilton's taxonomy of interestingness measures~\cite{robertj.hildermanKnowledgeDiscoveryMeasures2001}, we model analytical interest along four dimensions and map them to MDI's components:

\noindent \textbf{Structural interest.}
This dimension captures relationships between variables correlations, associations, and dependency structures. MDI's association component $A(t,q)$ aggregates evidence from association rules mined over the session history, using standard measures such as support, confidence, lift, and the J-measure~\cite{goodman1991rule}. Rules are weighted by a temporal decay factor so that recently reinforced patterns contribute more strongly than stale ones.


\noindent \textbf{Distributional interest.}
This dimension reflects anomalies and unusual distributional characteristics. We quantify diversity using established indices (e.g., Shannon entropy~\cite{shannon1948mathematical}, Simpson concentration, Gini coefficient~\cite{gini1921measurement}, Berger-Parker dominance~\cite{bergerDiversityPlanktonicForaminifera1970}, McIntosh diversity~\cite{mcintoshIndexDiversityRelation1967}). The diversity component $D(t,q)$ estimates how much adding tuple $t$ would increase the diversity of attribute-value distributions observed so far in the session.
%This dimension reflects anomalies and unusual distributional characteristics. We quantify diversity using a small set of standard indices (e.g., entropy and inequality measures) and define $D(t,q)$ as the estimated contribution of tuple $t$ to the diversity of attribute-value distributions observed so far.

\noindent \textbf{Temporal interest.}
Analytical goals evolve over the course of a session. Temporal cohesion measures~\cite{mannila1997discovery} and sequential pattern mining concepts inform how we track persistent investigation threads across queries. In MDI, this is reflected in the temporal decay applied to rules and distributions: patterns that continue to be supported by new queries retain high influence, while those not revisited fade over time.


\noindent \textbf{Compositional interest.}
%Analysts often care about natural groupings or contrasts between sub-populations. Cluster validity measures such as the silhouette coefficient and Calinski–Harabasz index~\cite{kaufman2009finding} inform MDI's novelty component $N(t,q)$, which promotes tuples whose attribute combinations are under-represented in the session history, following the inverse-frequency principle~\cite{sparckjones1972statistical}. This encourages exploration of complementary or surprising regions of the data space.
Analysts often care about natural groupings or contrasts between sub-populations. Inspired by cluster validity measures, MDI's novelty component $N(t,q)$ promotes tuples whose attribute combinations are under-represented in the session history, following the inverse-frequency principle~\cite{sparckjones1972statistical}.


%RBG important for the vision, one instantiation but could be more
These four dimensions need not be instantiated exactly as in MDI; they define a design space for implicit-interest models combining structural, distributional, temporal, and compositional cues.

\vspace{-1.5em}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Temporal prediction task.}
Given an analyst's query history $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_t$, we aim to predict tuples the analyst will focus on in query $Q_{t+g}$ for prediction gaps $g \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 10\}$. We treat tuples that reappear in $Q_{t+g}$ as relevant (Eq.~\eqref{eq:relevance}) and evaluate recommenders by how well their rankings anticipate future overlap.

We evaluate on three complementary benchmarks:

\begin{itemize} [leftmargin=1em, topsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
    \item \textbf{SIMBA drill-down benchmark.} Realistic visualisation-driven sessions generated using SIMBA~\cite{lenzi2019simba} over SDSS~\cite{york_sloan_2000}, with progressive drill-down producing high inter-query overlap. Results for this benchmark were presented in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} (Table~\ref{tab:simba-results}).

  \item \textbf{SkyServer SQL-log benchmark.} Real SQL sessions derived from the SkyServer usage study of Raddick et al.~\cite{raddickTenYearsSkyserver2014}. We sample 10 candidate sessions uniformly at random, and report the completed benchmark outputs (8 sessions across all gaps, plus one short session available only at gap $=1$). To keep experiments reproducible and independent of the live SkyServer backend, we evaluate against pre-materialised query result sets for these sessions rather than reissuing the queries to the full deployment.

  \item \textbf{Controlled adversarial benchmark.} Synthetic sessions constructed using the workload-recommender alignment recipe from Section~\ref{sec:theory:recipe}. A 9-column categorical fact table embeds five association rules at 85\% confidence, with noise rows biased toward common values. Query sequences alternate between large (${\sim}150$ rows, noise-dominated) and small (${\sim}45$ rows, rule-dominated) results, yielding sparse signal, strong frequency skew, homogeneous noise, and sufficiently long sessions for temporal accumulation, conditions under which our framework predicts association-aware, history-based methods are informative.

\end{itemize}
\noindent The SIMBA benchmark produced one session of 20~queries with an average result size of 156~rows; the sampled SkyServer benchmark contributes 981~queries across the 10 sampled sessions before gap filtering; and the adversarial benchmark comprised three sessions averaging 28~queries and 109~rows per result.

We compare six recommendation approaches, all operating in a memory-based setting where they select tuples from current query results to predict future results. The recommendation budget at each step is set to the size of the future result to ensure fair comparison:

\begin{itemize} [leftmargin=1em, topsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{MDI (Ours).} Association rules ($\alpha=0.5$), diversity ($\beta=0.3$), novelty ($\gamma=0.2$), temporal decay ($\lambda_r = 0.05$).
  \item \textbf{Frequency.} Ranks by historical attribute-value frequency counts.
  \item \textbf{Clustering.} $k$-means ($k=5$) on normalised attributes, selecting cluster representatives.
  \item \textbf{Similarity.} Cosine similarity to the mean encoding of the current result set.
  \item \textbf{Sampling.} Stratified random sampling weighted by historical frequency.
  \item \textbf{Random.} Uniform random selection from current query results.
\end{itemize}


\noindent We evaluate using precision, recall, F1, and overlap (fraction of recommended tuples in future results), all with exact tuple matching.

\subsection{Discussion}

Our experiments support three key observations.

\noindent \fcirc{1}\textbf{Realistic visualisation-driven workloads cannot differentiate recommenders.}
On SIMBA drill-down sessions, all six methods achieve similar F1 scores (Table~\ref{tab:simba-results}). Progressive drill-down produces high inter-query overlap, making any sample from current results likely to appear in future results. Evaluating solely on such workloads risks overstating the performance of simple baselines and obscuring advances in interest modeling.


\noindent \fcirc{2}\textbf{Real query logs can be misaligned with the recommendation task.}
The sampled SkyServer sessions are authentic and historically grounded, but they test a different failure mode: many queries are object-ID lookups rather than open-ended exploration. As a result, tuple-level prediction from current results becomes nearly impossible and all methods collapse toward zero. Ecological validity alone is therefore insufficient: realistic logs may be \emph{too} heterogeneous or too weakly aligned with the target recommendation task to yield informative comparisons.


\noindent \fcirc{3}\textbf{Controlled benchmarks reveal genuine capabilities.}
When workload parameters follow the alignment recipe from Section~\ref{sec:theory:recipe}, sparse rule signal (low effective $\rho$), strong frequency skew (high $\xi$), homogeneous noise, and sufficient history, MDI's temporal accumulation yields a clear 36\% F1 improvement over random (Table~\ref{tab:adversarial-results}). This demonstrates that MDI is genuinely exploiting association structure rather than merely benefiting from overlap artifacts.

\textbf{Adaptive interest models and richer signals.}
EDA interests can shift dramatically within a single session as new patterns are discovered. Developing online algorithms that adapt in real time, while integrating richer implicit signals from visual interfaces (e.g., clicks, hovers, dwell times) with structural signals from query logs, is a key challenge.

\textbf{Interpretability, trust, and transfer.}
Because EDA recommendations directly influence analytical conclusions, systems must provide transparent explanations of why specific tuples or regions are recommended and how they might bias exploration. At the same time, analysts often work across multiple datasets and domains, raising questions about how exploration patterns transfer and how to mitigate cold-start effects through transfer or meta-learning.

\textbf{Extending alignment and validating impact.}
Extending the workload-recommender alignment framework to neural and language model-based recommenders, and learning to weight different interestingness dimensions automatically, are important open problems. Ultimately, longitudinal user studies are needed to assess whether implicit recommendation systems enhance or constrain analytical discovery compared to explicit-feedback baselines.
