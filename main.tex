\begin{abstract}

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is central to modern data science, yet current EDA systems rely on explicit user feedback, example validation and active learning that disrupts cognitive flow and captures only a narrow view of analytical interest. We argue that implicit behavioral signals from natural exploration provide a richer, more authentic basis for recommendation. However, evaluating implicit recommenders poses its own challenge: realistic workloads produce high inter-query overlap that renders all methods statistically indistinguishable, masking differences in recommender capability. This paper makes four contributions: (1) a systematic critique of explicit feedback paradigms in EDA, showing their cognitive overhead and analytical limitations; (2) a workload recommender alignment theory that formally characterises when each class of recommender succeeds or fails, yielding a prescriptive recipe for constructing controlled benchmarks; (3) a Multi-Dimensional Interestingness (MDI) recommender that combines association rules, diversity measures, and novelty detection through temporal accumulation; and (4) a complementary evaluation methodology pairing realistic visualization-driven sessions (ecological validity) with a controlled adversarial benchmark (construct validity). On the adversarial benchmark, MDI achieves 36\% higher F1 than random selection, while on realistic drill-down workloads all methods perform similarly demonstrating that principled evaluation methodology is as important as recommender design.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The volume of enterprise data continues to grow rapidly, with recent projections estimating global data creation will exceed 180 zettabytes by 2025~\cite{idc2019data}. This growth has placed Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) at the centre of modern data science workflows. Yet despite decades of database research, the fundamental interaction paradigms for data exploration remain largely unchanged.

The database community has developed interactive exploration systems that enable users to navigate large datasets through visual interfaces, query-by-example paradigms, and active learning~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016, shen2014discovering, rezig2021dice}. These represent important advances over traditional query-answer models. However, they continue to rely on explicit user feedback tuple rankings, example validation, Boolean membership queries which impose cognitive overhead that disrupts analytical flow~\cite{monsell2003task, rubinstein2001executive}. Moreover, they optimise for similarity rather than analytical interest and typically assume interestingness can be captured by a single measure, despite empirical evidence that analytical interests are contextual and multi-dimensional~\cite{somechPredictingWhatInteresting2019, chansonInterestingnessMeasuresExploratory2025a}.

We envision EDA systems that use implicit behavioural signals such asthe queries analysts write, the results they inspect and the sequence of their actions to understand and predict analytical interest without interrupting exploration. Implicit feedback has proven effective in other recommendation domains~\cite{hu2008collaborative, kelly2003implicit, koren2009collaborative}, and the temporal richness of query sequences provides a natural basis for modelling evolving analytical goals.

However, a second challenge arises: \emph{how should we evaluate implicit recommenders for EDA?} We show that realistic visualization-driven workloads produce high inter-query overlap due to progressive drill-down, making all recommenders from simple random selection to sophisticated methods statistically indistinguishable. This finding implies that prior evaluations using only realistic workloads may overstate baseline effectiveness while undervaluing capable methods. Principled evaluation methodology is therefore as important as recommender design itself.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A systematic critique of explicit feedback paradigms in EDA, demonstrating their cognitive overhead and analytical limitations (Section~\ref{sec:implicit}).
  \item A \emph{workload-recommender alignment theory} that formally characterises when each class of recommender succeeds or fails, yielding a prescriptive recipe for constructing controlled benchmarks that test specific recommender capabilities (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
  \item A Multi-Dimensional Interestingness (MDI) recommender that combines association rules, diversity measures, and novelty detection through temporal accumulation as one concrete instantiation of an association-aware recommender (Section~\ref{sec:mdi}).
  \item A complementary evaluation methodology pairing realistic sessions with a controlled adversarial benchmark, demonstrating both ecological and construct validity (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{From Explicit Feedback to Implicit Signals}
\label{sec:implicit}

\subsection{Limitations of Explicit Feedback in EDA}

Current EDA systems suffer from a fundamental design flaw: they assume analytical interest can be captured through similarity to previously explored data. Explore-by-example systems~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016, mottin2014exemplar, bonifati2014interactive} optimise for tuple similarity or pattern matching rather than analytical interest, conflating proximity in feature space with cognitive relevance. Query-by-example systems such as YMALDB~\cite{drosouYmaldbExploringRelational2013} require users to specify similarity criteria and provide explicit feedback on recommendation quality. Faceted search systems like DICE~\cite{rezig2021dice, el2016dice} enable structured exploration but depend on explicit validation of candidate results.

Empirical studies demonstrate that no single interestingness measure consistently predicts what analysts find interesting across different domains and analysis goals~\cite{somechPredictingWhatInteresting2019}. Experiments across financial, scientific, and social media datasets reveal that correlation-based measures perform well in some contexts while clustering-based measures excel in others~\cite{chansonInterestingnessMeasuresExploratory2025a}. This heterogeneity suggests that effective EDA systems must employ multi-dimensional interest modelling.

Despite their individual contributions, all existing interactive exploration systems share a common limitation: they require explicit feedback whether through tuple rankings~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016}, example validation~\cite{rezig2021dice}, Boolean membership queries~\cite{bonifati2014interactive}, or similarity assessments~\cite{mottin2014exemplar}. This requirement interrupts the natural flow of analytical reasoning and imposes cognitive overhead that scales poorly with exploration depth.

\subsection{Implicit Signals as a Richer Alternative}

We argue that implicit feedback from natural exploration behaviour provides a fundamentally richer and more reliable signal about analytical interest than explicit rankings or ratings. This assertion rests on three observations:

\textbf{Cognitive Flow Preservation.} Explicit feedback forces analysts to switch between exploration and evaluation modes. Studies in cognitive psychology demonstrate that such context switching imposes measurable cognitive overhead and reduces problem-solving effectiveness~\cite{monsell2003task, rubinstein2001executive}. Cognitive load theory~\cite{sweller1988cognitive} further explains that exploratory analysis already imposes substantial intrinsic load; adding explicit feedback requirements as extraneous load can overwhelm working memory. Implicit feedback preserves cognitive flow by inferring interest from naturally occurring behaviours.

\textbf{Behavioural Authenticity.} Implicit signals reflect genuine analytical decisions when an analyst formulates particular follow-up queries or investigates certain patterns, these behaviours represent authentic expressions of interest unconstrained by artificial rating scales~\cite{kelly2003implicit}.

\textbf{Temporal Richness.} Implicit feedback provides multi-scale temporal information about interest evolution~\cite{koren2009collaborative}. Short-term signals (which tuples are accessed first) reveal immediate priorities, while longer-term patterns (which investigation threads persist across queries) expose analytical goals. Pirolli and Card's information foraging theory~\cite{pirolli2005information} predicts that analysts follow information scent gradients, producing behavioural signals that reliably indicate information value.

\section{Why Evaluation Methodology Matters}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Developing implicit recommenders for EDA raises a critical question: how should we evaluate whether a recommender genuinely learns analytical patterns rather than benefiting from structural properties of the workload?

\subsection{The Overlap Problem in Realistic Workloads}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\label{fig:overlap-analysis}
\includegraphics[scale=0.6]{figures/overlap_analysis.pdf} 
\caption{Analysis of tuple overlap between consecutive query results in SDSS exploration sessions.}
\end{figure}

We use the SIMBA benchmark~\cite{lenzi2019simba} to generate realistic visualization-driven exploration sessions over the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) dataset~\cite{york_sloan_2000}. SIMBA produces SQL queries through a configurable action loop with progressive drill-down, filter, and link operations that narrow result ranges by a factor of $0.60$-$0.85$ per step.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap-analysis} reveals that these realistic sessions exhibit high inter-query overlap due to progressive narrowing. Table~\ref{tab:simba-results} confirms the consequence: when we evaluate six recommenders on SIMBA sessions, all achieve similar F1 scores (range: 0.266-0.335), with no clear winner. The similarity baseline actually achieves the highest F1. This is not a failure of any particular recommender but rather a property of the workload: high overlap means any subset of current results has a high probability of appearing in future results, making the prediction task trivially easy for all methods.

This finding has important implications: prior work evaluating recommenders exclusively on realistic workloads may overstate the effectiveness of simple baselines while undervaluing sophisticated approaches. Existing EDA benchmarks such as IDEBench~\cite{eichmannIDEBenchBenchmarkInteractive2020} were designed to measure DBMS performance under interactive exploration, not to differentiate recommender quality.

\subsection{The Need for Construct Validity in EDA Benchmarks}

To reason about benchmark adequacy, we distinguish between two forms of validity~\cite{shadish2002experimental}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ecological validity}: Do the benchmark sessions resemble real analyst behaviour? SIMBA provides this its progressive drill-down and visualization-driven queries are representative of actual analytical workflows.
  \item \textbf{Construct validity}: Does the benchmark actually test the capability it claims to measure? SIMBA \emph{lacks} this for recommender evaluation, since it cannot tell us \emph{why} a recommender succeeds the high overlap structure conflates genuine analytical pattern recognition with simple statistical coincidence.
\end{itemize}

The need for diagnostic benchmarks that test specific properties rather than only measuring overall performance on naturalistic data is well established in other fields. In computer vision, CLEVR~\cite{johnson2017clevr} introduced synthetic scenes to diagnose whether visual question-answering models perform compositional reasoning or exploit dataset biases. In NLP, CheckList~\cite{ribeiro2020beyond} provides behavioural tests that probe specific linguistic capabilities, and adversarial NLI benchmarks~\cite{nie2020adversarial} test whether entailment models rely on annotation artefacts.

We adopt the same principle for EDA recommender evaluation. Our adversarial benchmark (detailed in Section~\ref{sec:theory:recipe}) tests a specific \emph{property}: can the recommender detect association rules embedded in the data? This is a method-agnostic question any association-aware recommender should succeed, not just MDI. The benchmark is constructed by deriving conditions under which each class of baseline provably fails, then instantiating a workload that simultaneously satisfies all these conditions. We present this derivation as a general theoretical framework in the next section.

\section{Multi-Dimensional Interestingness Recommender}
\label{sec:mdi}

The theoretical framework in Section~\ref{sec:theory} defines the MDI scoring function formally (Eq.~\ref{eq:mdi-score}) and shows that its temporal accumulation property gives it an advantage over memoryless baselines when the workload contains sparse association-rule signal. Here we describe the intuition behind each component and the design choices involved.

\subsection{Four Dimensions of Analytical Interest}

Drawing on Hilderman and Hamilton's foundational taxonomy of interestingness measures~\cite{robertj.hildermanKnowledgeDiscoveryMeasures2001}, we model analytical interest along four dimensions:

\textbf{Structural Interest} captures relationships between variables correlations, associations, and dependency structures. The association component $A(t,q)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:Acomponent}) aggregates evidence from mined rules using confidence, support, lift, and the J-measure~\cite{goodman1991rule}, weighted by temporal decay to prioritise recently relevant patterns.

\textbf{Distributional Interest} captures anomalies and unusual distributional characteristics. We quantify diversity using established measures including Shannon entropy~\cite{shannon1948mathematical}, Simpson's concentration, the Gini coefficient~\cite{gini1921measurement}, the Berger-Parker dominance index~\cite{bergerDiversityPlanktonicForaminifera1970}, and the McIntosh diversity index~\cite{mcintoshIndexDiversityRelation1967}. The diversity component $D(t,q)$ measures how much adding tuple $t$ would increase distributional diversity in the session history.

\textbf{Temporal Interest} captures trends and change patterns. Temporal cohesion measures~\cite{mannila1997discovery} and sequential pattern mining reveal how investigation threads evolve across queries, integrated through the temporal decay weights $w_r(q)$ that ensure the model adapts to evolving interests.

\textbf{Compositional Interest} captures clustering structures and natural groupings. Cluster validity measures including the silhouette coefficient and Calinski-Harabasz index~\cite{kaufman2009finding} inform the novelty component $N(t,q)$, which promotes tuples whose attribute value combinations have appeared sparsely in the session history, following the inverse-frequency principle~\cite{sparckjones1972statistical}.

\subsection{Combining Components}

The MDI score combines association, diversity, and novelty with weights $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:mdi-score}). In our experiments we use $\alpha = 0.5$, $\beta = 0.3$, $\gamma = 0.2$ with temporal decay rate $\lambda_r = 0.05$. These are illustrative values; the theoretical analysis (Proposition~\ref{prop:temporal}) shows that MDI's qualitative advantage temporal accumulation of association evidence holds across a wide range of parameter settings. Association rules are mined from accumulated query history using standard frequent itemset mining~\cite{aggarwalNewApproachOnline2001} with minimum support threshold $\theta_s = 0.08$.

\section{Complementary Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Temporal Prediction Task.} Given an analyst's query history $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_t$, predict which tuples the analyst will focus on in query $Q_{t+g}$ for prediction gaps $g \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 10\}$.

We evaluate on two complementary benchmarks:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SIMBA Drilldown Benchmark.} Realistic visualization-driven sessions generated using SIMBA~\cite{lenzi2019simba} over SDSS~\cite{york_sloan_2000}, with progressive drill-down producing high inter-query overlap. Results for this benchmark were presented in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} (Table~\ref{tab:simba-results}).
  \item \textbf{Controlled Adversarial Benchmark.} Synthetic sessions constructed using the prescriptive recipe from Section~\ref{sec:theory:recipe}. A 9-column categorical fact table embeds 5 association rules at 85\% confidence, with noise rows biased toward common values via active perturbation. Query sequences alternate between large (${\sim}150$ rows, noise-dominated) and small (${\sim}45$ rows, rule-dominated) results, simultaneously satisfying the failure conditions for all baseline recommenders (Propositions~\ref{prop:freq} \ref{prop:rand}).
\end{itemize}

We compare six recommendation approaches, all operating in a memory-based setting where they select tuples from the current query results to predict future results, using the future result size as the recommendation budget to ensure fair comparison:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MDI (Ours).} Association rules ($\alpha=0.5$), diversity ($\beta=0.3$), novelty ($\gamma=0.2$), temporal decay ($\lambda_r = 0.05$).
  \item \textbf{Frequency.} Ranks by historical attribute-value frequency counts.
  \item \textbf{Clustering.} K-means ($k=5$) on normalised attributes, selecting cluster representatives.
  \item \textbf{Similarity.} Cosine similarity to the mean encoding of the current result set.
  \item \textbf{Sampling.} Stratified random sampling weighted by historical frequency.
  \item \textbf{Random.} Uniform random selection from current query results.
\end{itemize}

We evaluate using precision, recall, F1, and overlap (fraction of recommended tuples in future results), all with exact tuple matching.

Table~\ref{tab:adversarial-results} shows the results on the adversarial benchmark. MDI achieves an F1 of 0.260 at gap=1, outperforming the random baseline by 36\%. This advantage arises because MDI's temporal accumulation mechanism (Proposition~\ref{prop:temporal}) builds association evidence across queries, identifying rule-conforming tuples that baselines systematically miss. Frequency and similarity perform worst (F1 of 0.150 and 0.160), confirming the theoretical failure conditions: the benchmark's biased noise rows concentrate common values, causing these methods to rank noise above signal (Propositions~\ref{prop:freq} and~\ref{prop:sim}).

\subsection{Discussion}

Our experiments demonstrate three key findings.

First, \textit{realistic visualization-driven workloads cannot differentiate recommenders}. On SIMBA drilldown sessions, all six methods achieve similar F1 scores (Table~\ref{tab:simba-results}). This occurs because progressive drill-down produces high inter-query overlap, making any sample from current results likely to appear in future results. Prior work evaluating recommenders exclusively on realistic workloads may therefore overstate baseline effectiveness.

Second, \textit{controlled adversarial benchmarks reveal genuine recommender capabilities}. When workload parameters satisfy the failure conditions from Section~\ref{sec:theory:failures} sparse rule signal ($\rho \approx 0.22$), high frequency skew ($\xi = 4$), homogeneous noise ($\sigma^2_{\text{noise}} \leq 0.11$) MDI's temporal accumulation produces a clear 36\% F1 improvement over random (Table~\ref{tab:adversarial-results}). This validates the theoretical framework and demonstrates that MDI genuinely learns association patterns rather than exploiting overlap.

Third, \textit{the prediction task is fundamentally hard}. Even on the adversarial benchmark where MDI has a clear advantage, absolute F1 remains at 0.26 far from perfect prediction. This reflects the inherent difficulty of predicting tuple-level analyst focus from implicit signals alone, motivating continued research into more expressive interest models.

\textbf{Addressing the ``custom benchmark'' concern.} A natural question is whether the adversarial benchmark is designed to favour MDI. We argue it is not, for three reasons. First, Theorem~\ref{thm:recipe} shows that workloads favouring \emph{any} recommender including Frequency, Clustering, or Random can be constructed by choosing different parameter regimes. The adversarial benchmark is one principled instantiation, not a special case. Second, the benchmark tests a \emph{property} can the recommender detect associations? not a specific method. Any association-aware recommender (not just MDI) should outperform baselines on this benchmark. Third, the SIMBA evaluation provides the ecological complement: MDI performs comparably to other methods on realistic workloads, showing it does not sacrifice generality for adversarial performance. Together, the two benchmarks provide complementary evidence that neither could provide alone.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Interactive Exploration and Active Learning}

The database community has long recognised limitations of traditional query-answer paradigms for exploratory analysis. Online aggregation~\cite{hellerstein1999online} established the principle that exploration systems must balance accuracy with responsiveness. Query-by-example systems such as YMALDB~\cite{drosouYmaldbExploringRelational2013} recommend similar data based on explicit preferences. Explore-by-example approaches~\cite{dimitriadouAIDEActiveLearningBased2016} use active learning to refine understanding through tuple rankings. Query discovery systems~\cite{shen2014discovering} find project-join queries from example tuples. Exemplar query systems~\cite{mottin2014exemplar} use queries as examples of desired results. Interactive join inference~\cite{bonifati2014interactive} discovers join predicates through Boolean membership queries. Drill-down operators~\cite{kalininInteractiveDataExploration2014} and query steering~\cite{ugurcetintemelQuerySteeringInteractive2013} provide structured navigation. Relevance feedback techniques from information retrieval~\cite{rocchio1971relevance} offer iterative refinement but are designed for text, not structured multidimensional data.

All these systems share a common requirement for explicit feedback, imposing cognitive overhead that our implicit approach avoids.

\subsection{Interestingness Measures and Pattern Mining}

Hilderman and Hamilton~\cite{robertj.hildermanKnowledgeDiscoveryMeasures2001} provide a foundational taxonomy distinguishing between pattern-based and summary-based interestingness measures. Somech et al.~\cite{somechPredictingWhatInteresting2019} demonstrate empirically that no single measure consistently predicts analyst interest, supporting our multi-dimensional approach. Recent work has explored learning interestingness from user studies~\cite{das2018learning} or domain-specific rules~\cite{ruiz2014learning}, but these still rely on manual interaction and fail to capture the dynamic, contextual nature of interest evolution.

\subsection{Implicit Feedback in Recommendation Systems}

Collaborative filtering with implicit feedback~\cite{hu2008collaborative, koren2009matrix} demonstrates the superiority of implicit signals in traditional domains. Temporal dynamics modelling~\cite{koren2009collaborative, wang2018modeling} addresses evolving preferences. ReCRec~\cite{lin2024recrec} reasons about causes behind implicit feedback, distinguishing genuine disinterest from lack of exposure. However, all existing implicit feedback approaches assume predefined item catalogues and stable preferences assumptions that break down in EDA where the tuple space is dynamically generated and interests evolve rapidly based on discoveries.

\subsection{Benchmarks for Data Exploration}

Existing EDA benchmarks focus primarily on DBMS performance rather than recommender quality. IDEBench~\cite{eichmannIDEBenchBenchmarkInteractive2020} measures system responsiveness under interactive workloads. SIMBA~\cite{lenzi2019simba} generates realistic visualization-driven sessions but, as we demonstrate in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, cannot differentiate recommenders due to high inter-query overlap.

Our approach of constructing diagnostic benchmarks that test specific properties follows established methodology in other fields. CLEVR~\cite{johnson2017clevr} diagnoses compositional reasoning in visual QA by using synthetic scenes with known structure. CheckList~\cite{ribeiro2020beyond} tests specific linguistic capabilities of NLP models through controlled perturbations. Adversarial NLI benchmarks~\cite{nie2020adversarial} expose whether entailment models rely on annotation artefacts. We apply the same principle to EDA: our adversarial benchmark tests whether recommenders can detect embedded associations when heuristic shortcuts are systematically removed.

\section{Research Challenges and Future Directions}

The vision outlined in this paper opens several research directions.

\textbf{Real-Time Interest Model Adaptation.} EDA presents a fundamentally different challenge from traditional recommendation: user interests can shift dramatically within a single session. Developing online learning algorithms that rapidly adapt while maintaining computational efficiency requires advances in incremental pattern mining and novel approaches to balancing exploration versus exploitation.

\textbf{Interpretability and Trust.} Unlike entertainment recommendation, EDA recommendations directly impact analytical conclusions. Analysts must understand why specific tuples are recommended and how recommendation algorithms might bias their discoveries, requiring domain-specific explanation frameworks beyond traditional explainable AI.

\textbf{Cross-Domain Interest Transfer.} Analysts often work across multiple datasets. Understanding how exploration patterns transfer across data contexts could reduce cold-start problems and improve accuracy, requiring transfer learning approaches specifically designed for analytical interests.

\textbf{Extending the Theoretical Framework.} The workload-recommender alignment theory (Section~\ref{sec:theory}) currently covers six recommender classes. Extending the framework to characterise neural and language-model-based recommenders deriving their failure conditions and prescribing benchmarks is an important open problem. Additionally, adaptive weight learning could automatically tune MDI's parameters ($\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$) based on workload characteristics rather than using fixed values.

\textbf{User Studies.} Longitudinal studies tracking analytical discovery patterns over extended sessions are needed to evaluate whether implicit recommendation systems enhance or constrain analytical discovery compared to traditional approaches.

% SECTION 9: CONCLUSION
%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

This paper argues for two paradigm shifts in how database systems support exploratory data analysis. The first is a shift from explicit to implicit feedback: rather than interrupting analysts with ranking requests, systems should infer interest from natural exploration behaviour. The second is a shift from ecological-only to construct-valid evaluation: realistic workloads are necessary but not sufficient for assessing recommender quality, and controlled benchmarks that test specific capabilities are essential.

Our workload-recommender alignment theory provides a general tool for reasoning about when and why different recommenders succeed, yielding a prescriptive recipe for benchmark construction applicable beyond the specific recommenders studied here. The MDI recommender and complementary evaluation demonstrate one instantiation of this approach, achieving a 36\% F1 improvement over random on the adversarial benchmark while performing comparably on realistic workloads.

The research challenges we identify real-time adaptation, interpretability, cross-domain transfer, and theoretical extensions represent opportunities for the database community at the intersection of data management, machine learning, and human-computer interaction. As data volumes grow and analytical complexity increases, building systems intelligent enough to understand analytical intent from natural exploration behaviour becomes increasingly critical.
